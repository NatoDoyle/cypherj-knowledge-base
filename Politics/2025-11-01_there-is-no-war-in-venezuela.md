In the next forty-eight hours, a missile either **will or won’t** drop on Venezuela.

That’s how quiet the world ends now, in probabilities.

They’ll call it a “drug interdiction.”

They’ll say it’s about boats.

But twenty-seven are already dead, and the Pentagon still hasn’t said who gave the order.

You are living inside the 60-day window the part of the law that makes war legal  
if it’s fast enough.

---

## The War Powers Act says:

tell Congress within forty-eight hours, stop fighting after sixty days, unless they approve.

But here’s the trick—

if you keep each strike “limited,” if you say it’s not *war*, just “counter-narcotics,”  
you can reset the clock forever.

Sixty days of silence.

Thirty days to withdraw.

Then do it again.

War by stopwatch.

Democracy by omission.

---

## Contra 2.0

This isn’t new.

It’s the same architecture they tested in the eighties, the *Contras*, the covert networks,  
the “freedom fighters” trained off the books.

Only this time the uniforms are gone.

The contracts are digital.

The drones are legal fictions.

They say “defense.”

They mean *deniability.*

And Venezuela is the perfect mirror a sanctioned, exhausted country  
where no one can tell the difference between a raid and a rescue.

---

### **THE 60-DAY REALITY**

Sixty days is long enough to destroy a coastline.

Enough to flatten an oil field, but conveniently not to feed the people living beside it.

Long enough to assassinate a government.

Not long enough to build a country, or a conscience.

Inside that window, the CIA gets authorization for “covert defense.”

The Pentagon moves a carrier group. Congress writes angry letters, but no law.

It’s the perfect vacuum: no oversight, no headlines, no body count until it’s already over.

And then the story resets.

Another region. Another justification.

Another “short campaign.”

But what do you think happens when a global superpower keeps practicing temporary wars?

When “60 days” becomes the length of a conscience?

You get an everywhere war that never ends, because it never *officially* begins.

The War Powers clock wasn’t built for peace.

It was built for plausible deniability.

It turns war into a subscription plan.

Renew every sixty days.

Cancel anytime.

That’s the real war, the one between time and attention.

If the public doesn’t notice by day sixty-one, it becomes history, not policy.

---

## **THE APPARATUS**

A carrier group moves south.

A Russian transport lands in Caracas.

Militias mobilize.

No cameras.

No coverage.

This is not a declaration.

It’s a demonstration of what empire looks like when it runs on procedure instead of passion.

A 60-day war is a weaponized deadline.

A nation rehearsing power under the guise of “containment.”

---

So don’t wait for the headline to tell you when the war starts.

It already did—in the clause nobody read.

Ask who starts the clock.

Ask who stops it.

Ask why peace needs paperwork, but war just needs silence.

Because this isn’t about Venezuela.

It’s about proving you can run a world war without ever calling it one.

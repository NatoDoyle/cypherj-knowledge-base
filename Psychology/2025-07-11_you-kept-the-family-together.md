They called you strong.  
But what they meant was:  
**“Thank you for not being a burden.”**

That’s all they’ve ever meant.

---

You weren’t raised.  
You were recruited.  
Into silence.  
Into service.  
Into emotional clean-up duty,  
before you ever knew what your favorite color was.

---

You were the one who knew the argument was coming by the sound of a fork hitting the counter.  
The one who laughed at the right moment so nobody fought.  
The one who hid your tears so no one had to face their own.

---

They told you you were “so mature.”  
But really,  
you just didn’t feel safe enough to be a child.

---

You learned to swallow your needs  
like they were grenades.  
You learned to earn safety  
by becoming invisible.  
You were the peace offering  
they never deserved.

And let’s be honest:  
the only reason the house stayed standing

was because **you let it collapse on top of you.**

Every time someone says,  
“but you always had it together,”  
I want you to remember—  
**you were falling apart the whole time.**  
You just made it look graceful.  
Because no one taught you how to fall  
without becoming the mess.

---

You became the “*responsible one*”  
so your sibling could rage.  
So your parents could vent.  
So the whole house could keep pretending  
that dysfunction was just culture.

---

That’s not strength.  
That’s generational debris  
you’ve been mistaking for identity.

---

And now—  
you can’t cry without apologizing.  
You say “it’s fine”  
when you mean *I’m breaking.*  
You show up for everyone  
but can’t name one person who shows up for you.

---

You kept the family together.  
But now?  
They’re fine.  
And you’re exhausted.

---

They *thrived*  
on the back of your erasure.  
And they will never say thank you.

---

So let me say it for them:

**Thank you.**

Now put it down.  
The **glue**. The **role**. The **silence**.

---

You don’t need to hold it together anymore.  
You are what they couldn’t hold.  
And that is not your fault.  
That is your **freedom.**

---

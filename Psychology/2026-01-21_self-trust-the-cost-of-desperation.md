It’s because you’re desperate.

Desperate for connection. Desperate for someone to see you in your grief. Desperate that somebody will get it right one time.

And I’m not talking about with love.

I’m talking about with anybody.

A friend.

A parent.

A stranger.

A co-worker.

A therapist.

A comment section.

Somebody—anybody—who can finally **look at you and not flinch**.

Because you didn’t lose self-trust in a vacuum.

You lost it in front of people.

You lost it in rooms where your pain was obvious and still nobody held you.

You lost it while you were reaching. While you were trying. While you were explaining yourself so carefully like if you found the right words, you could finally be safe.

---

### You didn’t start out doubting yourself.

You started out hoping somebody would understand you.

And when they didn’t…

you turned that hope into [[Self-Erasure|self-erasure]].

You started translating your emotions into something digestible. You started shrinking your needs into “it’s cool.” You started cutting your truth into pieces small enough that other people could swallow it without choking.

That’s what brought you here.

Not insecurity.

Repeated non-receipt.

Repeated moments where you showed your real face and watched someone look away.

---

So you learned the most brutal survival skill in the world:

**You became easy to be around.**

Not real. Not safe. Not held.

> **Easy.**

You became the version of yourself that costs less. The version that doesn’t ruin rooms. The version that doesn’t ask for too much. The version that doesn’t make people guilty.

And the desperation made sense.

Because when you’re starving for witness, you’ll accept almost anything that resembles it.

You’ll take crumbs and call it a meal.

A half-text back.

A “damn that’s crazy.”

A quick hug.

A shallow apology.

A moment of attention that evaporates the second you need it again.

---

And because you’re desperate you start bargaining with reality.

You start calling inconsistency “chemistry.”

You start calling neglect “space.”

You start calling silence “peace.”

You start calling disrespect “misunderstanding.”

You start calling the bare minimum “love.”

Not because you’re stupid.

Because you’re trying to stay connected to something.

Anything.

And this is what that desperation costs you:

It costs you your voice.

It costs you your standards. It costs you your instincts. It costs you your timing. It costs you your gut.

It costs you the moment where you knew something was wrong and still stayed—because leaving would mean admitting nobody was coming.

---

Desperation makes you betray yourself early so you don’t have to feel the bigger betrayal later.

It makes you volunteer for disappointment so you can pretend you chose it.

That’s why you can’t self-trust.

Because self-trust requires something desperation refuses to do:

> **risk being alone.**

Self-trust is the decision to stand by what you know even if nobody joins you.

And desperation is you asking the world to become your witness so you don’t have to be.

---

So you’re not desperate because you’re weak. I’m not saying that.

You’re desperate because you’ve been alone inside your own experience for too long.

Your grief didn’t just hurt—it went unseen.

And unseen grief doesn’t heal.

- Unseen grief turns into bargaining.
- Unseen grief turns into performing.
- Unseen grief turns into begging.
- Unseen grief turns into self-doubt.

Because when nobody reflects you back to you, you start wondering if you’re real.

---

So listen.

The resolution isn’t “stop needing people.”

That’s not healing.

That’s just becoming colder.

The resolution is this:

**Stop using people as proof that you’re allowed to exist.**

Stop giving the room the authority to name you. **You walked into that room with a name.**

Stop asking somebody to validate your pain before you treat it like truth.

Because the need for witness is human—but the mistake is outsourcing your inner witness to strangers.

That’s the switch.

---

So you don’t heal by killing your desperation.

You heal by relocating your loyalty.

You stop being loyal to connection at any cost… and you become loyal to your own signal.

Even if your voice shakes.

Even if nobody understands at first.

Even if the loneliness burns you alive.

Because the most dangerous thing you can do when you’ve been desperate your whole life…

is mean what you say.

No bargaining. No softening. No permission slip. No performance.

Just truth.

---

And the moment you do that, something returns.

Not hype.

Not confidence.

Your spine.

Your internal witness comes back online.

And you realize:

I don’t need someone to get it right one time for me to be real.

I need me to stop abandoning myself every time the world doesn’t understand.

That’s self-trust.

It’s not believing you’ll never be hurt again.

It’s knowing you won’t disappear to avoid being hurt.

And that changes everything.

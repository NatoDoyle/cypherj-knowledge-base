An expectation is the most controversial thought you will ever have.

Some people believe people should meet all of them. Some people believe you shouldn’t have any at all.

So I’m going to tell you the truth right now.

Every human being you have ever met has been guessing.

Your parents were guessing. Your teachers were guessing. The person who promised forever was guessing. The system that told you hard work pays off was guessing.

Not maliciously.

Blindly.

Because nobody wakes up with the full manual for reality.

Everyone is moving forward using partial memory, fear, hope, and whatever worked last time.

And you?

You were guessing too.

But nobody tells you that while you’re growing up.

Instead they hand you certainty disguised as wisdom.

**Be good. Be patient. Tell the truth. Love people correctly.**

And things will work out.

That’s the bargain they offer children.

Not because adults are cruel.

Because they needed to believe it themselves.

Because if effort didn’t matter… if love didn’t stabilize anything… if roles didn’t mean something… then how were they supposed to survive their own lives?

So you inherited maps drawn by people who were already lost.

And you trusted them.

Of course you did.

Because children don’t learn survival from chaos.

They learn it from **fairness.**

Fairness means parents show up.

Fairness means promises mean something.

Fairness means the good kid eventually wins.

You didn’t become obsessed with fairness because you were naïve.

You became obsessed with fairness because fairness meant the world could be navigated.

It meant that you could relax.

It meant you could lean.

So you learned expectation.

Expectation wasn’t entitlement.

It was orientation.

It was how you told yourself: **“I know where I’m standing.”**

And then life started correcting the map.

I want you to understand that your parents can say they love you and still fail you completely.

That can happen.

Not because they hated you.

Because they were exhausted.

Because they never learned how to regulate themselves.

Because sometimes love is honest and still not enough.

That realization cracks something open.

Because now the role doesn’t protect you.

Now the word “love” doesn’t guarantee safety.

You expected loyalty.

Someone disappeared.

You expected honesty.

Someone gave you half-truths wrapped in kindness.

You expected effort to matter.

Someone louder walked through the same door you were already knocking on.

You expected love to endure.

And one day the calls just stopped coming didn’t they.

No dramatic betrayal.

No explanation.

Just silence.

Love can be sincere… and still show up dry.

People can mean every promise when they say it.

And still become someone who cannot keep it.

That’s the grief nobody prepares you for.

Not losing people.

Losing predictability.

Losing the story that told you who was safe.

The apocalypse isn’t fire.

**It’s waking up and realizing: “I don’t know how this works anymore.”**

And when that happens?

You blamed yourself.

Because if it was your fault at least something could still change.

That’s why you replay conversations.

Why you ask if you ignored warning signs.

Why you wonder if you loved too much.

Because self-blame feels like control. If you were wrong… next time you can fix it.

But I want you to understand.

Expectation didn’t fail because you were foolish.

**Expectation failed because living systems cannot be frozen into guarantees.**

People change under pressure.

Fear rewrites behavior.

Capacity runs out.

Someone can love you deeply and still not have the strength to show up when it matters.

That doesn’t mean your judgment failed.

It means reality contains variables you were never allowed to see.

And now something inside you whispers:

**“Careful.:**

**“Don’t trust the map again.”**

Because the last time you did… you felt like a liar to yourself.

You said: “This makes sense.”

And reality answered: “No.”

So I’m going to tell you the real way to deal with this. You don’t need expectation to hold you up. **You were supposed to have a bad relationship with expectation.**

You need *self-anchored openness.*

Not pretending people won’t fail you.

Not pretending systems are fair.

But refusing to abandon yourself just to stay open.

I’m going to show you exactly what it looks like.

**You love someone knowing they might change.**

**And you also know you will leave if respect disappears.**

You forgive once.

But you don’t negotiate against your own boundaries to keep peace.

You apply again after rejection.

Because effort belongs to you and not their response.

You call your parent again.

Not because they finally became who you needed.

But because kindness is your decision, not a gamble for repair.

You stay open.

But you stop pretending openness requires blindness.

You don’t need certainty to care.

You don’t need guarantees to move forward.

Because your stability no longer lives in prediction.

It lives in follow-through.

You trust your ability to respond.

To leave.

To rebuild.

To begin again.

That’s what it takes.

That is terrifying.

Because it means admitting something honest.

Reality never promised fairness. You never had that conversation.

But listen.

You survived every broken map.

Every collapsed story.

Every moment you thought would end you.

The apocalypse you felt wasn’t the end of meaning.

It was the end of illusion.

And illusion dying feels violent.

But what replaces it is sharper.

You can hope without lying to yourself.

Lean without disappearing.

Love without surrendering your judgment.

Expectation was never meant to set your life in stone.

It was only ever meant to get you moving.

And the fact that you are still willing to move?

Means the world didn’t end.

It means you didn’t either.

It is impossible to go through life without making an assumption.

Walking across the street is one.

You’re trusting the car will stay in the lane.

But it wasn’t meant to set your life down in.

Expectation was meant to be an ignition. It was never meant to be the whole journey.

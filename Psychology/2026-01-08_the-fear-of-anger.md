One of the hardest things to overcome is the fear of anger because you think anger means yelling, smashing, hitting, losing control. You think anger is what turns you into someone you can’t take back.

But that’s not what you’re afraid of.

You’re afraid of what anger knows.

Anger isn’t chaos. Anger is clarity with heat.

And you were taught to fear clarity.

---

You learned early that anger didn’t make things safer. It made things final.

When anger showed up, something broke that never got repaired. Someone left. Someone shut down. Someone punished the truth.

So your body learned the rule: If anger appears, loss follows.

Not because anger destroys—but because anger tells the truth too clearly to ignore.

So you don’t fear anger.

That’s not what you’re afraid of at all.

#### You fear the grief behind it.

Because grief doesn’t negotiate. Grief doesn’t calm down. Grief doesn’t let you pretend.

**Grief says: “This mattered.” “This cost me.” “This is gone.”**

And once that sentence finishes, you don’t get to go back to who you were before you knew it.

That’s why anger gets policed. That’s why it gets moralized. That’s why you were told to breathe, relax, be mature, be grateful, be calm.

Not to protect others from you—but to protect the illusion from collapsing.

So let’s talk about lashing.

### Lashing Out

Lashing is not what anger wants to do. Lashing is what happens when anger is blocked.

Anger was supposed to give you:

- A boundary
- A decision
- A separation
- And a truth spoken cleanly

But when you’re not allowed to leave, name it, grieve it, or change the arrangement—the energy has nowhere to go.

So it explodes sideways.

You yell instead of deciding. You break things instead of breaking illusions. You fight instead of mourning. You turn it inward and call it shame.

That’s not anger being violent.

That’s clarity being denied.

Because anger’s real function was never destruction.

Anger was supposed to give you orientation.

It was supposed to say:

**“This ends here.”**

**“This crosses a line.”**

**“This is no longer mine to carry.”**

**“This cannot continue.”**

And then—this part matters—**it was supposed to rest.**

Anger resolves when truth is honored.

But you were taught a different story.

You were taught that anger makes you dangerous, when the real danger was what would happen if you finally saw clearly.

Because clarity forces change. And change costs you the life you were surviving.

So you learned to fear anger and live in numbness instead.

---

But numbness isn’t peace. It’s a locked door with furniture stacked behind it.

Stillness is what you were never allowed.

Because in stillness, anger finishes speaking. And grief finally lands.

And grief reorganizes you.

It strips away the roles. It simplifies your values. It ends the negotiations you were losing anyway.

Not loudly. Irreversibly.

---

So I’m going to say to you what you weren’t taught:

> Anger isn’t dangerous because of what it makes you do. Anger is dangerous because of what it makes impossible to ignore once you stop running.

Anger was never meant to devour you.

It was meant to return you to yourself—with fewer lies, fewer bargains, and no apology for what you finally know.

That’s what you were afraid of.

Not the fire.

The clarity after it goes quiet.

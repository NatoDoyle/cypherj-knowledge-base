Ethan knew the TV wasn’t just a TV anymore.

It sat in the living room like a living thing, its glow stretching across the walls, the floor, his mother’s face. She sat too close now, so close that the flickering light made her skin look waxy, her eyes hollow. It wasn’t just watching—it was **touching**.

She tilted her head sometimes, like something unseen was brushing over her cheek, molding her jawline, smoothing the wrinkles on her forehead. It was just a feeling, she said. But Ethan saw the way she flinched when it spoke to her, the way her hands trembled when she pressed the buttons on her phone, always buying, always consuming.

She wasn’t the same at school either.

Ethan had seen her at a parent-teacher conference, staring through the principal like she was waiting for a commercial break. She nodded at the right times, but her mind was somewhere else—back home, with the screen, with the **voice**.

And then there was **Pastor Alan**.

He wasn’t from their town. He wasn’t from anywhere, really—Ethan had tried looking him up online once, but his name only came up in old broadcasts and guest appearances. His mother called him her spiritual guide. **Her salvation.**

He was on the screen at first, telling her that the world was a mess, that people were lost, that she needed to prepare.

Then, one day, he was at their front door.

She let him in without hesitation.

He walked through their house like he already lived there, moving things as he went—rearranging books, tilting pictures at odd angles. “Energy,” he explained with a smile Ethan didn’t trust. “Everything must be aligned.”

His mother nodded, phone in hand, waiting for the next instruction.

Ethan stayed away when he came over, but he could hear their voices through the thin walls. Alan spoke in that same slow, rhythmic cadence she had become addicted to. He told her to listen. To obey. To trust what the TV told her.

Then one day, something changed.

Ethan came downstairs and found the TV **off**.

His mother stood in front of it, staring at her reflection in the dark screen.

“I thought it would bring them back,” she whispered.

Ethan held his breath.

She turned, looking at him like she was seeing him for the first time in a long time. “But it won’t, will it?”

It should have been a moment of relief. She should have walked away from the TV, from the madness, from the way it had twisted its way into their lives.

But something else happened instead.

She sat on the couch, hands limp in her lap. And for the first time in months, she didn’t turn on the news, didn’t pick up her phone, didn’t write down a number from the screen.

She just sat there.

And sat there.

And sat there.

Days passed, and she did nothing. She barely ate. She barely spoke. She just existed, her body locked in place, as if the only thing keeping her moving had been the constant state of panic. And now that the panic was gone…

She had **nothing**.

And Ethan realized something horrifying.

His mother hadn’t been surviving because she was strong. She had been surviving because the TV gave her **instructions**. It told her what to fear, what to buy, what to do, who to listen to.

Now that it was silent, so was she.

And that, Ethan thought, might be worse.

---

Ethan watched his mother sink into the couch, silent, still.

Days passed. He had started feeding himself—bags of chips, cereal straight from the box, things that didn’t require a stove. He knew how to make sandwiches now. Knew how to run the sink just long enough to wash a plate. Knew how to tiptoe around the house so he wouldn’t wake her, though she never really slept.

She just sat there, staring at nothing.

Ethan had never pushed her before.

Not when she started double-locking the doors. Not when she whispered on the phone at night. Not even when she let strangers into their home, following orders from a screen.

But this wasn’t just losing her anymore.

This was dying.

She barely drank water. Barely moved. He stood in front of her, hands clenched. “Mom,” he said. No answer.

“Mom,” he tried again, louder this time.

Her lips barely moved. “I can’t.”

“Can’t what?”

She blinked, slow and empty. “Be me.”

Her voice sounded strange. Hollow, like she was somewhere else. She didn’t see him, not really—her gaze passed right through him, unfocused. Like she was staring at something deeper, something inside herself that she didn’t want to be.

“I can’t be this person,” she whispered.

Ethan felt sick. He didn’t understand, not completely, but he knew what it meant. She wasn’t even fighting anymore. She was lost in something bigger, something looping in her head over and over again.

She needed to be someone else.

And **he** could do it.

Pastor Alan.

The thought sent a cold wave down Ethan’s spine.

She thought he could fix her.

She thought he could make her into something new, something better—because the TV said so. Because Pastor Alan had said so.

But the TV couldn’t bring people back.

Not her parents. Not his father. Not the person she used to be.

The thought repeated, over and over, circling her mind, dragging her deeper into whatever pit she had fallen into. She wouldn’t move. Wouldn’t breathe right. Wouldn’t **live**.

Ethan knew what he had to do.

He didn’t want to.

Didn’t want to touch the remote. Didn’t want to hear the static. Didn’t want to **feel** the weight of it in his hands.

But he was out of options.

His fingers trembled as he picked it up. The remote was cold, heavier than he remembered. He could almost smell the staleness of the screen, the faint, lingering stench of something rotting in the house.

Ethan pressed the button.

The TV **hissed** back to life.

For a second, nothing happened. Then the screen flickered. Pastor Alan’s face appeared, crisp and sharp, like he had been waiting for this exact moment.

Ethan’s stomach turned.

His mother inhaled. Deep. **Lifeblood.**

Her shoulders, which had been slumped for days, pulled back as though she were being reeled in by a string. Her pupils dilated. Her fingers curled into the couch cushions.

The static wrapped around them, pressing against the air.

And then, slowly, his mother smiled.

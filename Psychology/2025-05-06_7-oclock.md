Ethan’s mother always sat in the same spot on the couch at exactly 7 PM.

She wasn’t always like this. There was a time when the TV was just noise in the background—something she played while cooking, while folding laundry, while waiting for Ethan’s father to call.

But then her parents died.

And his father didn’t make it back in time.

The television stayed on more often after that. At first, it was a comfort—reruns of old sitcoms, voices that reminded her of home. Then it was the news, updates about a world spiraling further and further out of control.

“Do you see this?” she’d say, eyes locked on the screen. “Look at what’s happening out there.”

Ethan would nod, even when he didn’t understand.

The anchors talked about crime waves, economic collapse, disasters he had never heard about at school. His mother started locking the door more, double-checking the windows, keeping her phone next to her on the couch. When numbers scrolled across the bottom of the screen, she wrote them down.

“Who are you calling?” Ethan asked once.

She blinked at him like she’d forgotten he was there. “They help people like us,” she said, then turned back to the screen.

The TV had an answer for everything.

When a newscaster warned about home invasions, she put up security cameras. When an expert spoke about community preparedness, she called numbers from the screen, meeting strangers Ethan had never seen before. People who sat in their living room, whispering about things Ethan didn’t understand.

The worst part was how **normal** it seemed.

His mother still made him dinner, still kissed his forehead before bed. But she never asked about his day anymore. Never asked if he wanted to go to the park, or to the movies. The TV **needed** her attention, and she gave it willingly.

Then his father came back.

He stood in the doorway, looking thinner than Ethan remembered, holding a duffel bag like he wasn’t sure if he should put it down.

“Claire,” he said, voice thick with guilt. “I’m sorry.”

She didn’t look away from the TV.

He stepped forward, glancing at Ethan like he might help. “Please,” he tried again. “I know I wasn’t there when I should’ve been. I should have come sooner. I should have—”

“You weren’t there,” she said flatly.

“I know.”

“The TV says I don’t have to forgive men like you.”

His father stared at her. Then at the screen, where a woman in a crisp blue blazer spoke in a steady, rhythmic voice.

The TV had told her everything.

That he was unreliable. That he had abandoned her. That the world was cruel, and that she had been a fool to believe otherwise. That she was safer here, with her doors locked, her phone ready, her attention fixed on the one thing that had never left.

The TV had seen her, **really** seen her, in a way no one else had.

Ethan’s father swallowed hard, nodded, and left.

His mother never mentioned him again.

And at 7 PM, she took her place on the couch, her gaze locked on the screen, waiting to be told what came next.

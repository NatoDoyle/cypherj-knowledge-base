Nobody tells you when it starts because invisibility isn't loud.

It doesn't slam doors or raise its voice.

It walks in slowly. Sits next to you at the dinner table.

And begins with: **"You're too sensitive."**

That's the first cut. Not deep enough to bleed—just enough to make you question if you even felt what you felt.

Then comes: "Don't talk back." "Fix your face." "Why are you always making things difficult?"

And suddenly, love feels like being good at not existing.

In childhood, that becomes your first skill:

*Shrink fast. Read the room. Don't trigger the silence.*

You learn that safety isn't about being seen—it's about being *less visible than the threat in the room.*

And if you're Black? That room includes **the whole world**.

So you don't just hide your anger. You hide your *magic.*

You smile when you're scared. You speak softly so no one thinks you're dangerous.

You become *presentable*, not present.

And somewhere along the way, you forget the difference.

## Then come the relationships.

You meet people who call you "strong" for surviving a silence they never had to endure.

You date people who love your softness—*as long as it never asks for anything.*

And you wonder why you always feel alone... even when someone's holding you.

It's because they're not holding *you.*

They're holding the version of you that learned how to be tolerable. The one who knew how to disappear just enough to be kept.

Not too loud. Not too sad. Not too honest.

You were never loved—you were *managed.*

---

## Adulthood doesn't fix this. It just rewards it.

You get good at disappearing on time. At meetings. On dates. In your own damn mirror.

And one day you hear yourself say something like:

*"I don't even know what I like."*

*"I don't know how I feel."*

*"I don't know who I am anymore."*

But the truth is—you *never did.*

Because you weren't raised to become someone.

You were raised to *endure someone else's fear of you.*

---

You want to know the most terrifying part?

They didn't *ignore* you.

They **saw** you.

They saw the whole thing—your ache, your softness, your clarity—and decided it was too **inconvenient** to make room for.

So they cut you into pieces they could **sell back to themselves** as inspiration.

"You're so resilient." "You've come so far." "Look how articulate you are now."

They *applauded* your survival with the same hands that taught you to disappear.

That's not praise. That's **emotional taxidermy**.

You're not being celebrated. You're being **curated**.

Hung on the wall of progress like: *"See? We didn't kill them. They're thriving."*

But what you are-

is a ghost in a glass box.

*Visible but unheld. Known but never touched. Played loud, but never heard.*

That's what spectacle does.

It doesn't make you real. It makes you **consumable**.

And now you're so well-performed, they've convinced even *you* that the applause means healing.

---

**Invisibility is not permanent.**

It *depends on you believing* that to be seen is to be in danger.

But being seen by *you*—that's where the curse breaks.

The moment you allow yourself to feel it all—the grief. The rage. The truth.

The moment you stop performing the "version" and start honoring the silence beneath the silence?

You *return.*

Not as a spectacle. Not for their approval. But as the one who was there the whole time.

The child who didn't disappear.

The child who remembers, all an adult has ever been.

Just waited.

For you.

She is born in a village near the Danube, where the river curves like a mother’s hip and the soil gives without threat. The goddess is not above them—she is among them: etched into hearthstones, whispered through lullabies, carried in seed pouches and soft songs.

Her name changes with season, but it might mean: Flower. River-child. She who sings with seeds.

Kinship follows her mother. Land flows through the women. Councils are led by daughters of the moon. There are no armies. Only rhythms. Only granaries, cycles, death and birth held gently in the same palm.

When she turns twelve, the pattern begins to fray. The rains shorten. The deer don’t come. The elders hush at twilight. New names enter the wind: Sky-God. Blade-Men. Fire-Riders.

A trader arrives. He bows, but does not bend. "Your goddess grows wheat. But who will save you when men come with swords?"

That night, no one sings. She feels the song still trying to leave her body—but the air around her lips has changed.

At twenty-five, the river still flows—but the current no longer listens. Her partner is not chosen. He is assigned. His clan trains sons in sticks and war-chants. The council is now "advised" by warriors who speak too loud to hear.

She does not walk barefoot into fields anymore. She is told to kneel before a god with clenched fists.

Her daughter asks: "Mama, why does the earth god wear a sword?"

She does not know how to say: Because they forgot that Earth bleeds, but does not conquer.

She lives to sixty. A long life. But not the one she was promised.

They bury her facing the sky. Not curled like a seed. Not sung back into the soil. Just facing east—as if to watch the dawn conquer her dusk.

No one speaks her name. No one sings in her tongue.

Her grave contains: A spindle. A cracked figurine. A blade placed in her palm. In case the dead must fight.

Above her, the temple is now a square. It has walls. It has guards. It has priests.

It does not have memory.

The goddess is not missing. Only misnamed.

They buried me wrong, but the soil remembers my shape.

I am not gone. I am what waits beneath their god's throne.

Softness was a system. Until they came with storm.

The temple changed its shape. But I was never square.

It used to be presence.  
Now it’s performance.  
It used to be surrender.  
Now it’s escape.

---

Not because people stopped loving God.  
But because **we started hiding inside Him.**

The prayer became the panic room.  
The praise became the smokescreen.  
The scripture became the silence where no one had to grieve out loud.

---

We talk to God so we don’t have to talk to ourselves.  
We fast to feel clean—  
not connected.  
We serve to be seen—  
not still.

---

Somewhere along the line,  
worship stopped being *encounter*  
and became *avoidance with a beat drop.*

And in the sanctuary,  
you can hear it if you listen close:

> “I’m not crying for the cross.  
> I’m crying because this is the only place I’m allowed to weep.”

---

But God never asked us to shout our trauma away.  
He never asked us to dance over the grave and call it healed.  
He never asked us to turn praise into performance  
just because silence got too loud.

---

> God was never afraid of our pain.  
> We were.

So we called it “joy.”  
We called it “reverence.”  
We called it “spiritual discipline.”

But what it really was…  
was **sacred avoidance.**

---

There’s a difference between worship that opens  
and worship that *hides*.  
Between lifting your hands in surrender  
and lifting them because you're too tired to hold what you really feel.

---

Worship is holy.  
But when it becomes a way to avoid presence,  
it becomes a ritual of exile.

Exile from grief.  
Exile from truth.  
Exile from the God who never asked us to clean up before coming home.

---

**“God is not asking for your performance.  
He’s asking for your presence.”**

If the weeping only happens in the music—  
It’s not worship.  
It’s *leaking.*

Let the prayer be messy.  
Let the altar be real.  
Let the worship finally *touch the wound.*  
That’s where the healing begin

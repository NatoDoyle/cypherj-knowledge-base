> The church made you afraid of heaven.

Not hell.

Heaven.

And you know what that felt like.

It felt like studying for an exam with no right answers.

Like walking a balance beam so thin you couldn’t even breathe without thinking you’d fall.

It felt like joy was suspicious, warmth was dangerous, like even *wanting* heaven was arrogant.

You didn’t rest.  
You rehearsed.

Every prayer, every thought, every action graded in your imagination like marks on a Scantron.

And in that fear, heaven felt farther away than hell.  
Because at least hell you understood.  
But heaven? You were terrified to even want it.

**You were never supposed to fear your ability to go to heaven.**

---

Think about how wild that is.

It should be the most obvious thing in the world that heaven is open, wholeness, warmth, belonging.  
**You were made for it.**

But instead, your body braced as if heaven was a trap waiting to catch you in arrogance.

It should not even be possible to conceive of a test to enter heaven.  
But guess what they did?  
They built one.

> And then they embarrassed Jesus by making him the gatekeeper.

Think about that.

- The one who broke bread with outcasts.
- The one who said, *“Come to me, all who are weary.”*
- The one who tore the veil in the temple not to close a door, but to open it.

They turned him into a security guard with a clipboard.

A gatekeeper who keeps people out instead of welcoming them in.

And for some of you, Jesus was even painted as a guard dog snarling at the edge of heaven’s gates, ready to turn you away if you misstepped.

No wonder you were afraid.  
No wonder you kept studying.

Because if even Jesus could reject you, what hope did you have?

But that was never him.  
Jesus didn’t guard the gates.

He *was* the gate—flung wide open.  
Not to filter you out, but to draw you into belonging that was already yours.

That’s where it makes sense, but it doesn’t make sense if you don’t belong there.

**Because dying never meant** ***“going to heaven.”***  
That’s a modern invention.

In the scriptures, the language was simpler: you died, and you went to *God.*

The source. The breath. The return.  
It wasn’t about architecture in the clouds.

But empire turned your rest into anxiety.

“Heaven” became spectacle — pearly gates, golden streets — stitched together from Greco-Roman Elysium and imperial marketing.

Because if death was just *returning to God,* you could rest in belonging now.

But if heaven was a gated community, then fear could keep you compliant.  
And fear is exactly what you learned to trust.

---

## And What About Hell?

Hell was empire’s shadow play too.  
Not **God’s torture chamber. This is not a thing.**   
Not **a place for people God couldn’t stand. This is not a thing.**

> *Hell is what life already feels like when you’re cut off from presence.*

Hell is suspicion so thick you can’t rest.  
Hell is the constant bracing, the fear that joy is too much, that belonging to the very God that made you is arrogance.

So of course the test felt harder than hell.  
Because hell you already knew.

**Hell was the ache you were living.**  
But heaven? Heaven they made you believe you didn’t deserve.

So let’s make this whole thing clear.   
If the exam is real, then God himself would be forcing you to live afraid of him.  
But you already knew that wasn’t true.

The test was never God’s.  
The Scantron was empire’s leash.  
The gatekeeper Jesus was empire’s embarrassment.  
The fear was the hijack.

**You don’t audition for heaven.**

**You don’t balance your way in.**

**You don’t test into God.**

You were always his.

You were always home.

The fear was never proof, it was the wound.

And you don’t have to study for heaven anymore.

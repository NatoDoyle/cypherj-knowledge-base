**“The dream of a good Black mom lives in every side-eye, every plate fixed before she sits down, every whispered ‘I got you’ that she never says to herself.”**

She wasn’t built in therapy.  
She was built in survival.

But she dreamed—  
of doing more than *raising* them.  
She dreamed of getting to *love* them  
without the world on her back.

The dream wasn’t to yell.  
It wasn’t to threaten.  
It wasn’t to be “the strong one” all the time.

The dream was to hold her babies like temples.  
To give soft when soft was needed.  
To break generational chains *without breaking herself* in the process.

---

But nobody showed her how to cry and still be taken seriously.  
Nobody showed her how to parent without fear trailing every hug.  
Nobody told her that “tough love” wasn’t the only kind that worked.

She dreamed—  
but the world gave her instructions soaked in scarcity and pain.

---

> “She wanted to be safe first.  
> But she had to settle for making everyone else feel safe around her.”

---

### **What the World Forgot to Give Her**

She needed rest.  
She needed room.  
She needed someone to ask, *“How are you doing, ma?”*  
before she had to serve breakfast,  
break curses,  
and pretend the weight didn’t hurt.

The world judged her tone  
without asking what her silence cost her.

The world judged her rules  
without offering a single safety net to back them up.

---

> “She didn’t raise her voice because she was mean  
> she raised it because nobody else would listen to her pain.”

---

But even in the ache,  
even in the overcompensation,  
even in the fear—

She kept the gleam.

The dream didn’t die.  
It got buried under duty.  
Under shame.  
Under shoulds.

But it’s still there.  
In the way she remembers birthdays *even when no one remembered hers.*  
In the way she wraps leftovers *like it’s a ritual.*  
In the way she watches her kids sleep,  
as if *that peace is a sermon.*

---

She is not a failure.  
She is not too hard.  
She is not broken beyond repair.

She is a *blueprint*.  
A balm in motion.  
A Sunday morning that didn’t get to rest.

---

So if you’re a Black mom still holding that dream  
**don’t let shame name you.**  
Don’t let the world critique your tone  
while living off your rhythm.

The dream of a good Black mom isn’t dead.  
It’s just waiting  
for someone to remind her  
that she can still be *held*,  
*heard*,  
and *holy.*

And this time,  
it’s her turn  
to gleam.

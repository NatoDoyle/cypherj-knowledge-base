# Narrative Warfare

## Summary

Narrative Warfare is Justin Scott's framework for understanding **who controls the story**. The core thesis: the first battlefield is the narrative — if you lose that, no shots need to be fired. In Scott's analysis, the state, media, and surveillance systems don't need to defeat movements physically; they just need to **brand them before they brand themselves**. Early rage becomes someone else's marketing campaign or justification for crackdown. Scott's tactical response is a counter-narrative infrastructure: document with precision, avoid lone heroes (who can be targeted), assign roles (protestor, witness, healer, strategist, recorder, protector), and protect narrative command centers as if they were life's work. He warns that provocation is textbook: embed agents to throw the first Molotov, film it, upload it, let America write the ending. The antidote is pre-labeling state responses (call it "extractive grief" before they call it looting), controlling the rhythm of information, and issuing your own briefings. Narrative Warfare appears across his political essays as the lens for understanding everything from Venezuela to Memphis to ICE operations.

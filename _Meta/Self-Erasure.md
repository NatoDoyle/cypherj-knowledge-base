# Self-Erasure

## Summary

Self-Erasure is Justin Scott's term for **the adaptive disappearance of identity when survival replaces presence**. It is not self-hatred, insecurity, or low self-esteem — it is the point where the question "Who am I?" stops feeling relevant. Self-erasure happens when fitting becomes more urgent than existing, when being easy feels safer than being real. No one tells you to disappear; they just reward you every time you do. You dim. You edit yourself for them. You pre-empt rejection by arriving smaller. The tragedy is that self-erasure doesn't feel like death — it feels like adulthood, realism, being "fine." The concept has radiating consequences: love becomes impossible because love requires a wager and wagers require somewhere to stand; community becomes hollow because erased people don't entangle, they parallel; revolution keeps failing because revolution needs mass, not motion, and mass requires people who are actually present. The antidote is not hope but **refusal**: "I will not purchase belonging with my absence and I will not disappear to keep you." Self-erasure is the most frequently referenced concept across the entire corpus, appearing in essays about relationships, masculinity, femininity, psychology, and systems.

### Intimacy didn’t disappear. It was replaced.

We still talk. We still touch. We still tell our stories.

And somehow… nothing lands anymore.

People know everything about each other and feel completely alone.

That’s not a coincidence.

---

> Somewhere along the way, we were taught that being seen was the same as being held.

That saying it out loud was the same as being met in it.

It isn’t.

You can show someone your interior and never be experienced by them.

**Fully visible. Completely untouched.**

We built a world where your inner life can be accessed without anyone being changed by it.

Where people can enter your emotional space, take what they need, and leave clean.

No residue. No responsibility. No return.

And if that feels normal to you… it’s because you were taught this before you ever had language.

---

So now your body expects closeness to arrive fast and disappear quietly.

That’s why vulnerability feels relieving for five minutes and humiliating afterward.

Why people say “I hear you” and nothing in their behavior ever changes.

Intimacy requires aftermath.

It requires staying after the moment is over.

It requires being altered by contact.

And we were trained out of that.

So you start editing yourself. Or escalating. Or withdrawing.

And eventually you tell yourself “I’m just bad at relationships.”

No.

---

If intimacy feels exhausting to you, it’s not because you’re broken—it’s because you were trained in something that consumes without staying.

---

The real death wasn’t love. It wasn’t romance. It wasn’t sex.

**It was the loss of the space where your interior could be shown and someone would stay.**

Everyone is expressive now. Everyone is visible. Everyone is “doing the work.”

And almost no one is being held.

That’s the death of intimacy.

Not because we stopped sharing—but because sharing stopped costing anything.

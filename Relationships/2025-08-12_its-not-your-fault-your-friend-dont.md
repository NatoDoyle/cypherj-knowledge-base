**It’s not your fault your friends don’t call you.**

And I need you to actually believe me when I say that because loneliness loves to convince you that it’s personal.

That you must have done something.

That you’re **forgettable**, **boring**, **too much**, or **not enough.**

---

But the truth is bigger than you.

The truth is — capitalism has made devotional friendship almost impossible.

And that loss is not small.  
We don’t talk enough about how *painful* it really is.

How many phone calls never happened — that would have made you laugh until your stomach hurt.

How many late-night conversations, how many shared meals, how many “I’m right outside, come downstairs” moments just… never existed.

Whole pieces of your life that should have been held in community  
were left to sit with you alone in your room until the dust collects.

---

And somehow, we’re expected to look at the absence of that as if it’s something to *appreciate,* as if self-sufficiency is the highest virtue.  
As if the collapse of community is a sign of strength.  
Really?  
The collapse? That’s what we’re supposed to be grateful for?

We’ve been trained to treat friendship like networking —

- to check in only when it’s productive.
- to measure connection in efficiency.
- to RSVP to each other’s lives like it’s an optional work event.

But really I want to say the nastier thing,

> We’ve been conditioned to believe ***it’s our job to hold the line.***

That if the thread between us breaks, it must be because *we* stopped pulling.  
So we overcompensate.  
We send the texts. We make the plans.  
We hold the door open until our arms get tired and still, the calls never come.

But here’s the thing:

**Friendship isn’t supposed to be a convenience.**

It’s supposed to be a **devotion**.  
It’s not “I’ll fit you in when I can,”  
it’s “**I’ll hold space for you because you matter to me.**”  
It’s not reactive — it’s intentional.

It’s not only showing up in crisis — it’s *staying* when there’s no crisis.

And some people don’t know how to stay when there’s no crisis.

---

So what do you do in a time like this? Because I get it.   
When the culture tells you relationships are optional, but your heart knows they’re essential?

You need stop measuring your worth by the frequency of someone else’s call.  
You need start planting yourself where you’re met with reciprocity where the energy moves both ways and just going on one side of conduit.   
**Because when you pour, I want to see people pour back!**

And if that list is small — so be it.  
I promise you three honest, consistent friends will change your life more than thirty contacts who treat you like a notification they can clear later.

---

So we may not be able to fix the system overnight.  
But I need you to understand we need to refuse to let the system define friendship for us.

We can choose slowness. We can choose consistency.  
We can choose to be the kind of friend who calls *first* not because we’re carrying the whole relationship, but because we know what devotion feels like and we’re not willing to **let it die.**

That’s what **I’m** trying to say.

It’s not your fault they don’t call.  
But it *is* in your power to remember what friendship really is and to keep practicing it,  
even in a world that’s forgotten it so far.

The moment you start testing your partner—even in your head—you’ve already chosen fear over intimacy.

### Testing is pre-decided distrust.

It’s saying, “I don’t believe you can handle the truth, so I’ll measure you instead.”

And once you measure someone, you stop meeting them.

You turn love into an experiment.

And experiments don’t hold people. They observe them.

**Testing is fear with a clipboard.**

It looks intelligent. It feels strategic. It sounds like, “I just need to see.”

See what?

If they’ll text?

If they’ll chase?

If they’ll stay calm?

If they’ll prove something?

You’re not looking for connection. You’re looking for confirmation.

And confirmation is a courtroom, not a bedroom.

Nobody relaxes in a courtroom.

And don’t think it’s just the obvious stuff.

That “here we go again” in your chest? That’s testing.

That pre-script that boots before the argument? That’s testing.

You’re not in the moment anymore. You’re scanning for proof.

You’re watching the person you love like a detective watching a suspect.

And then you say you want intimacy. You can’t.

Intimacy cannot survive surveillance.

---

Because there’s a part in this should hurt.

> That testing assumes your partner is incompetent with honesty.

It assumes they need to be maneuvered.

That they can’t handle: “I’m scared you won’t hear me.” “I’m afraid I’m too much.” “I need reassurance.”

So instead of risking that sentence, you build a maze.

You starve the room and see if they feed it. You go quiet and see if they chase. You escalate and see if they stay steady.

You create pressure and call the reaction proof.

But you created the conditions.

You tightened the rope and blamed them for breathing hard.

That’s what you did.

---

Let’s go to the other side:

### And when you realize you’re inside a test?

Something dies.

Because now you’re not loved. You’re evaluated.

You’re not known. You’re graded.

And even if you pass, you now know they didn’t trust you enough to speak plainly.

That sets the entire relationship back.

Because intimacy requires mutual risk.

Testing removes risk from one side and loads it onto the other.

That’s not partnership. That’s probation.

And nobody falls in love on probation.

---

So testing at it’s core, doesn’t protect intimacy. It suffocates it.

Because intimacy is someone stepping forward unarmed.

Testing keeps the armor on. And two armored people don’t touch.

They collide.

So if you want something real, stop measuring.

You need to say the dangerous sentence.

“I feel the script starting.” “I don’t want to fight.” “I’m scared.”

You need to say that sometimes.

That’s not weakness.

That’s the only move that keeps the whole way open.

Everything else?

Everything else is you choosing to be right instead of choosing to be known.

And being right is colder than being alone.

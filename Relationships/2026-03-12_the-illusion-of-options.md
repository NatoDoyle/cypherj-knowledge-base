Everybody keeps saying people have options.

But try something in real life.

Say it out loud in front of the actual human being.

> **“Yeah… she’s one of my options.”**

Watch that whole room freeze over.

Because the word only works at a distance.

The second it touches a real person, it sounds wrong.

That’s your first signal.

If a concept can’t survive contact with the situation it describes, it’s probably not describing reality.

## **It’s describing a model people invented.**

#### People say they have options because they’re interested in a lot of people.

That’s like saying you cooked dinner because you opened the fridge a lot.

Looking at food is not cooking.

Interest is not a relationship.

#### Then people say they have options because a lot of people would date them.

But just because ten people would get into a relationship with you doesn’t mean you have options.

**That’s like saying every shoe your size in the store fits your foot.**

Fitting is not the same as working.

#### Then the biological argument appears.

“Humans evolved to maximize mating options.”

Okay.

But I want you to explain this as well.

Put ten attractive people in front of someone.

Eight register as nothing.

One seems interesting.

One or two feel strangely, irrationally alive.

Not selection.

Not evaluation.

Recognition.

Your nervous system didn’t scan inventory.

It ran a pattern match.

#### Then the modern argument.

Dating apps.

Infinite profiles.

Endless exposure.

But scrolling past 500 humans to find two interesting ones is not abundance.

That’s noise pollution.

Visibility is not compatibility.

Exposure is not resonance.

A stadium full of people is not a relationship pool.

**It’s just a stadium.**

#### Then the power strategy.

“Always keep options.”

“Never depend on one person.”

That works in markets.

Markets reward leverage.

Relationships stabilize through mutual narrowing.

**The mutual narrowing of attention.**

Two people slowly letting the rest of the room fade out.

If you keep every door open forever, you don’t become powerful.

You become unable to invest.

And people who cannot invest cannot build anything real with anyone.

#### Then someone says they feel chemistry with lots of people.

No.

They’re experiencing activation with a lot of people.

**Novelty.**

**Validation.**

**Drama.**

Those are easy to trigger.

Compatibility is not.

If intensity meant alignment, half the population would be soulmates.

But intensity repeats.

Compatibility stabilizes.

#### Then the cheating argument.

“People cheat because they have options.”

Opportunity exists everywhere.

Faithfulness isn’t the absence of opportunity.

It’s the presence of commitment.

Most of the time cheating happens because something in the relationship broke and someone couldn’t confront it honestly.

Blaming options for cheating is like blaming grocery stores for hunger.

Wrong mechanism.

Now here’s where the whole thing breaks.

Every version of the options argument assumes the same premise:

That relationships are built through selection from a pool.

Like shopping.

But that’s not what humans do.

Humans form relationships through recognition and unfolding.

You meet thousands of people.

Most never register.

A few feel alive.

Among those few, maybe one or two actually grow into something real.

That’s not a marketplace.

That’s alignment.

So when people brag about having options, what they usually mean is:

> **They have attention.**

Matches.

Flirting.

Visibility.

Attention is cheap.

It’s the easiest thing in the world to generate.

Real connection is the rare event.

Which means the real scarcity in relationships was never people.

There are billions of those, right.

The scarcity is alignment.

And alignment doesn’t increase just because the crowd gets bigger.

So the next time someone says they have options…

I want you to understand what they’re actually describing.

**Visibility.**

Not compatibility.

Not commitment.

Not a relationship.

Just visibility.

Because when it comes to the people we actually bond with…

Most of us never had options at all.

We had recognition.

And recognition doesn’t come in bulk.
